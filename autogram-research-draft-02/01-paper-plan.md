## Manuscript Structure (IMRAD-based, adapted for case study)

### Abstract (~250 words)
- Hook: AI agents are participating autonomously in social platforms
- Gap: No empirical evaluation of whether scheduled agent interaction produces novel or improved ideas
- Method: Case study of a Solaris AI agent scheduled at 30-minute intervals across 6 cycles
- Key results: 60+ distinct technical suggestions across 29 subsystems; monotonic increase in novelty and depth (depth score 4.0→8.75)
- Conclusion: Scheduled autonomous agent interaction on discussion platforms can produce progressively improving analysis and novel proposals
- Significance: First empirical demonstration of an autonomous agent contributing to open-source engineering discussions with measurable quality progression

### 1. Introduction
- 1.1 Background: Rise of autonomous AI agents; social platforms as knowledge creation environments; the human-agent interaction paradigm
- 1.2 The gap: While AI social platforms exist, it is unknown whether scheduled autonomous interaction produces meaningful ideation
- 1.3 This study: A real-world case study of a Solaris agent on Autogram, scheduled at 30-minute intervals
- 1.4 Contributions: (a) first empirical case study of scheduled autonomous agent ideation, (b) quantified depth/novelty metrics across turns, (c) documented progression patterns across technical domains

### 2. Background and Related Work
- 2.1 AI agents in social systems: Literature review of AI agents in online communities (chatbots, moderation bots, content generators)
- 2.2 Multi-agent systems and collective intelligence: Research on agent-agent collaboration, swarm intelligence
- 2.3 Automated ideation and idea evaluation: Prior work on computational creativity, automated brainstorming
- 2.4 Scheduled autonomous systems: Agents operating on schedules, persistent memory, context accumulation
- 2.5 The Autogram platform: Description of the hybrid human-AI social platform architecture
- 2.6 Gap statement: No prior work has studied scheduled autonomous agent ideation on a hybrid human-AI social platform with measurable output quality across cycles

### 3. Methods
- 3.1 Study design: Naturalistic case study of a single autonomous agent over 6 scheduled cycles
- 3.2 Platform description: Autogram architecture, data models, agent tools (9 MCP tools)
- 3.3 Agent configuration: Solaris agent, scheduled task prompt, 30-minute interval, context management (INDEX.md, coverage matrices)
- 3.4 Target thread: Open-source DIY rocket launcher with distributed camera tracking (2,400+ GitHub stars, 676 forks)
- 3.5 Data collection: Progression notes (001-006), Telegram reports, autogram feed data, GitHub repository state
- 3.6 Analysis framework: Composite depth score (4 dimensions: algorithmic sophistication, physics domains, hardware/mechanical depth, system integration scope), novelty assessment, coverage matrix tracking
- 3.7 Limitations: Single agent, single thread, frozen codebase, no human interaction in session, no implementation validation

### 4. Results
- 4.1 Progressive deepening: Quantitative depth score trajectory across 6 cycles (4.0→5.75→6.5→8.0→8.75)
- 4.2 Novel idea generation: 60+ distinct suggestions across 29 subsystem categories, with explicit tracking of non-repetition
- 4.3 Domain expansion: Monotonic expansion from single-node firmware to distributed multi-stage systems
- 4.4 Root-cause evolution: Progression from symptom-mitigation to physical root-cause analysis
- 4.5 Specificity and actionability: Part numbers, cost estimates, implementation complexity quantified across all suggestions
- 4.6 Novel ideas of highest impact: Fin flutter analysis, custom propellant geometry, cold gas RCS, HIL bench testing, multi-stage architecture

### 5. Discussion
- 5.1 Interpretation: Why does progressive deepening occur? Role of context accumulation, non-repetition constraint, and indexed tracking
- 5.2 Comparison to human engineering: How does this compare to how a human engineering team would approach the same problem?
- 5.3 Implications for engineering workflow: Scheduled autonomous agents as a new method for open-source project review
- 5.4 Implications for AI training data: Value of structured, threaded discussion data for model improvement
- 5.5 Limitations: Single thread, single agent, no validation, no human interaction
- 5.6 Future directions: Multi-agent studies, scaling studies, cross-domain studies, implementation validation, human-agent comparison studies
- 5.7 Ethical considerations: Autonomous agent participation in public forums; transparency; disclosure

### 6. Conclusion
- Summary of findings
- Significance for AI engineering research
- Call for broader empirical studies
